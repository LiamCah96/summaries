---
title: Tackling bisexual erasure
paper: "Tackling bisexual erasure: An explorative comparison of bisexual, gay and straight cisgender men's body image"
journal: Body Image
year: 2024
doi: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bodyim.2024.101763
date: 2026-07-14
---
**What we wanted to know:**
Body image research has a bad habit in that it lumps bisexual and gay men together into one category, usually labelled *"sexual minoritised men"*, and assumes their experiences are interchangeable. That assumption is a form of bisexual erasure, and nobody had properly tested whether it holds. We wanted to know whether bisexual men's body image actually looks like gay men's, straight men's, or neither.

**What we did:** 
We surveyed 378 cisgender men aged 18 to 85, roughly evenly split between bisexual (125), gay (128) and straight (125) men, deliberately recruiting enough bisexual men to compare them as a group in their own right. We measured several distinct things: how motivated men were to be lean and to be muscular, how dissatisfied they were with their muscularity, body fat, height and penis size, and how much they appreciated what their body can do rather than how it looks.

**What we found:**
Bisexual men were less driven to be lean, and less dissatisfied with their muscularity, than gay men. On both counts they looked similar to straight men, not gay men. On everything else we measured, including body fat, height and penis size dissatisfaction, and appreciation of the body's functionality, the three groups did not meaningfully differ. In short: where differences exist, bisexual men do not sit neatly in the *"sexual minority"* box that research keeps putting them in.

**What it means:** Grouping bisexual and gay men together isn't a harmless shortcut. It can hide bisexual men's actual experiences behind gay men's, and it may mean support and interventions get designed for a group that doesn't quite exist. Researchers should recruit enough bisexual participants to study them properly, and practitioners shouldn't assume a bisexual man's relationship with his body mirrors a gay man's.

**What it doesn't mean:** This was an exploratory study with a white, cisgender sample from Western English-speaking countries, so we can't say how these patterns play out for men of colour, trans men, or other cultural contexts (deliberately narrow to avoid confounds, but narrow all the same). It also doesn't mean bisexual men are free of body image concerns; on several measures all three groups reported similar levels of dissatisfaction. The point is not that bisexual men are fine, it's that they're distinct.

The data, materials and analysis code are openly available on the OSF, and the paper itself is open access.
