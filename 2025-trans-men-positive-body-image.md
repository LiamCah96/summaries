title: "\"I feel at home\": how trans men find peace with their bodies"
paper: "\"I feel good, I feel comfortable, I feel at home\": Understanding the role of positive body image manifestations, body neutrality and functionality appreciation for trans men"
journal: Body Image
year: 2025
doi: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bodyim.2025.101941
date: 2026-07-14
---

**What we wanted to know:** 

Research on trans men's body image has focused almost entirely on the negative: dissatisfaction, distress, dysphoria. Far less is known about the other side, how trans men come to feel comfortable, even at peace, with their bodies. We wanted to understand that journey in trans men's own words, including the role of concepts like body neutrality (not needing to love or hate your body) and functionality appreciation (valuing what your body can do over how it looks).

**What we did:** 

We conducted in-depth, semi-structured interviews with 20 trans men from seven Global North countries and analysed them using reflexive thematic analysis, an approach designed to find patterns of meaning across people's accounts while staying close to their actual experiences.

**What we found:** 

Societal expectations of masculinity, the tall, muscular, hyper-masculine ideal, initially made things worse for many participants, reinforcing body dissatisfaction and the painful sense of mismatch between body and identity. Over time, though, many participants redefined masculinity on their own terms, prioritising authenticity over conformity to rigid ideals. Gender-affirming care played a key role: by easing the distress of gender incongruence, it freed participants to disengage from unrealistic body standards rather than chase them. Many described arriving somewhere quietly remarkable: not loving every part of their body, but not at war with it either, appreciating it for what it does. As one participant put it, "I feel good, I feel comfortable, I feel at home."

**What it means:** 

Support for trans men's body image shouldn't stop at reducing dissatisfaction. Body neutrality and functionality appreciation offer realistic, reachable goals that don't demand body positivity from people for whom that may feel impossible. The findings also suggest gender-affirming care does more than align body with identity; it can change a person's whole relationship with body ideals. Clinicians and intervention designers should take note.

**What it doesn't mean:** 

This is qualitative research with 20 participants from Global North countries: its strength is depth, not breadth. It maps how these journeys can unfold, not how often they do, and it can't tell us what proportion of trans men reach this kind of peace or what predicts it. Those are questions for future quantitative work, which we're pursuing.

The paper is open access, and the anonymised interview data are archived with the UK Data Service.
