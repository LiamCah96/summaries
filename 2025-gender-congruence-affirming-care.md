---
title: How feeling at home in your gender shapes trans men's body satisfaction and quality of life
paper: "Understanding the role of gender congruence and affirming care in trans men's body image and quality of life"
journal: International Journal of Transgender Health
year: 2025
doi: https://doi.org/10.1080/26895269.2025.2483223
date: 2026-07-14
---

**What we wanted to know:** 

Gender congruence means the sense that your body and life align with your gender identity, that you feel at home in yourself. We know it matters for trans people's wellbeing in general, but surprisingly little research has asked how gender congruence, body satisfaction and quality of life fit together specifically for trans men, or which parts of life (physical health, psychological wellbeing, social relationships, environment) are most affected.

**What we did:** 

We surveyed 166 trans men aged 18 to 30, recruited from Global North countries with universal healthcare systems so that access to care was broadly comparable. Participants completed established measures of gender congruence, body satisfaction and quality of life. We then tested a statistical model of how these constructs relate to one another, controlling for age and satisfaction with gender transition, and explored the role of gender-affirming care such as hormones and chest surgery.

**What we found:** 

Trans men who felt greater gender congruence reported higher body satisfaction and better quality of life in the physical, psychological and environmental domains, though not the social domain. Body satisfaction was itself linked to better physical and psychological quality of life, and part of gender congruence's benefit appeared to flow through feeling better about one's body. Gender-affirming care was associated with greater gender congruence, and through that, indirectly, with greater body satisfaction.

**What it means:** 

Feeling congruent in your gender isn't a luxury; in our data it sits at the centre of trans men's body satisfaction and broader quality of life. The findings are consistent with gender-affirming care benefiting wellbeing partly by increasing gender congruence, and they suggest that support for trans men should attend to congruence and body satisfaction together rather than treating body image as an isolated issue.

**What it doesn't mean:** 

This is a cross-sectional study: a snapshot, not a film. It shows associations, not proof that one thing causes another. Our sample was restricted to white participants and drawn mostly from the UK, so the findings shouldn't be assumed to generalise across ethnicities, cultures or healthcare systems where access to affirming care differs. Two of the quality of life measures (social and environmental) also showed weaker reliability in this sample, so findings for those domains deserve extra caution.
