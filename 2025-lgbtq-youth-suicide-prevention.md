---
title: What protects LGBTQ+ young people in the UK from suicide, and what puts them at risk
paper: "Understanding protective and risk factors in preventing suicide among LGBTQ+ youth in the UK"
journal: International Journal of Mental Health
year: 2025
doi: https://doi.org/10.1080/00207411.2025.2575422
date: 2026-07-14
---

**What we wanted to know:** 

LGBTQ+ young people are more likely to attempt suicide than their cisgender, heterosexual peers, yet the UK's national suicide prevention efforts have done little to address this inequality specifically. Prevention needs evidence: which factors protect LGBTQ+ young people, and which put them at risk? Until now, no large-scale UK study had examined both together.

**What we did:** 

Working in collaboration with The Trevor Project, and with an LGBTQ+ youth advisory group involved throughout, we analysed data from an online survey of 9,666 LGBTQ+ young people aged 13 to 24 across the UK. We used regression analyses to test which factors were associated with suicidality, and machine learning (random forest models) to rank which factors mattered most.

**What we found:** 

On the protective side, a positive LGBTQ+ school environment and social support and acceptance were significantly associated with lower suicidality. On the risk side, experiences of victimisation and discrimination, and food insecurity, were significantly associated with higher suicidality. When we ranked all the predictors, the most important included having a trusted adult, experiences of LGBTQ+ victimisation, food insecurity, having been subjected to conversion therapy, and school-related factors.

**What it means:** 

The levers for prevention are identifiable and, crucially, actionable. Schools matter enormously: safe, affirming school environments are among the strongest protective factors we found. So do relationships, with a trusted adult ranking among the most important predictors of all. And some risks, such as food insecurity, sit well outside what mental health services traditionally address, which suggests suicide prevention for LGBTQ+ youth cannot be left to health services alone. We argue the next UK suicide prevention strategy needs to take these findings seriously.

**What it doesn't mean:** 

The study is cross-sectional, so it identifies associations rather than proving cause and effect. Recruitment through social media means young people without safe online access weren't reached, the sample skewed white, and trans women were underrepresented, so there is more to learn about the groups our data captured least well.
