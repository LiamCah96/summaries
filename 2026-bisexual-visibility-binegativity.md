---
title: Where anti-bisexual stigma comes from changes what it does
paper: "Sexual identity visibility, mental health, and body dissatisfaction in bisexual cisgender men: associations with straight and gay community bi-negativity"
journal: Psychology & Sexuality
year: 2026
doi: https://doi.org/10.1080/19419899.2025.2590708
date: 2026-07-14
---

**What we wanted to know:** 

Bisexual men face stigma from two directions at once: from straight communities and from within LGBTQ+ communities. Research usually measures this "bi-negativity" as one lump, without asking whether stigma from different sources does different damage. We wanted to disentangle the two, and to understand how each relates to bisexual men's openness about their identity, their mental health and wellbeing, and how they feel about their bodies.

**What we did:** 

In a preregistered study, 200 UK-based bisexual cisgender men completed measures of bi-negativity from straight and from gay communities, sexual identity visibility (how open they are about being bisexual), mental health and wellbeing, body dissatisfaction, and perceived stress from the gay community. We tested statistical models tracing the pathways between these experiences.

**What we found:** 

The two sources of stigma behaved quite differently. Bi-negativity from the straight community, which our participants experienced more strongly, was associated with poorer mental health and wellbeing, and through that, with greater body dissatisfaction. Bi-negativity from the gay community showed no direct link to mental health or body dissatisfaction. Instead, and against our predictions, it was associated with greater visibility as bisexual, which in turn was linked to better mental health and wellbeing. Being out, in other words, looked like a resilience pathway, not a cost.

**What it means:** 

Anti-bisexual stigma isn't one problem; it's at least two, and the straight community's version appears the more psychologically harmful in our data. That makes anti-stigma work aimed at wider society especially urgent, particularly in a UK climate that international rankings show is deteriorating for LGBTQ+ people. The findings also challenge the assumption that concealing a bisexual identity is self-protective: in our sample, visibility travelled with better mental health, not worse. Supporting bisexual men to be safely visible may be a genuinely protective intervention.

**What it doesn't mean:** 

The study is cross-sectional, so the arrows in our model are theoretically informed rather than causally proven; it remains plausible, for instance, that mental health and body dissatisfaction influence each other in both directions over time. Our sample was 80.5% white and all cisgender men, so these pathways may differ for bisexual people of colour, bisexual women, and non-binary bi+ people. And "visibility was associated with better mental health" is not the same as "everyone should come out": safety and context still matter enormously.

The preregistration, analysis code, materials and anonymised data are all openly available.
